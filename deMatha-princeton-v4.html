DeMatha Wootten Fast Break → Princeton 4-Out

Complete 8-Phase System

8 Phases

Outlet & Lanes

1
5 outlets to 1 on the near sideline. Whichever side 1 catches is the strong side — governs every downstream decision. Drill: rebounder calls "ball!" — 1 breaks immediately.
2
2 and 3 fill the sideline lanes. Destination is the baseline corner — not the wing. Run all the way. Cue: "corner or bust."
3
4 is the rim runner — middle lane, straight to the basket. The primary scoring threat on the break. Every other player's spacing is designed to protect this lane.
For ParentsThink of it like a football kickoff return: three lanes, three runners, nobody sharing a lane. The middle runner goes to the end zone. The sideline runners go to the back corners.

Push — Wings to Corners

1
1 attacks the middle immediately after catching. Never dribble up the sideline. The middle attack collapses any zone or man defense and opens the rim runner.
2
First look is always the rim runner (4). If 4 has a step and space, deliver the pass for the layup. This is the primary DeMatha fast break score.
3
2 and 3 sprint their sideline lanes all the way to the baseline corners — they do not slow at the wing. Even while the break is happening. "Don't watch — run."
4
5 trails and reads: Does 4 score? If yes, sprint for offensive glass. If no, slow to a read — they are the Princeton trailer piece.

PG → Strong Elbow → Opp Corner

1
No layup to 4. 1 looks to the strong-side corner (2). Pass and follow — after the pass, 1 follows to the strong-side elbow immediately.
2
The strong-side elbow is the read point. 1 arrives and reads: open for the return pass and drive? Yes → call for it. No → continue the cut through the paint.
3
From the elbow, 1 cuts hard to the opposite (left) corner. This clears the strong side and is the direct cue for 5 to fill the vacated slot.
🔗 The Structural Bridge1's elbow-to-opposite-corner path IS the transition from fast break to Princeton. The slot 1 vacates is exactly where 5 steps in. No stopping. No regrouping. One continuous movement.

5 Fills · 3 Lifts — Simultaneously

1
5 reads 1 cutting away. 5 flows directly into 1's vacated slot — the strong-side elbow/wing area above the arc, vertically aligned with the right elbow. 5 inherits 1's space exactly.
2
At the same moment, 3 lifts from the weak-side left corner to the opposite top slot — left elbow/wing above the arc. These two moves happen simultaneously as 1 cuts through.
3
2 holds the right corner. No shot → holds, waits for 5 to arrive, then swings ball to 5 to initiate the Princeton entry.
4
4 on the weak-side block — the only interior player in the 4-out set.
Drill Cue"1 goes down, 3 comes up — same time." Players must link these two movements. It's one trigger, not two separate actions.

Princeton 4-Out Set

1
Top slots aligned above elbows: 5 at top right (above right elbow), 3 at top left (above left elbow). Both inside the 3pt arc vertically, wide enough to shoot but not on the sideline.
2
Corners at baseline: 2 in right baseline corner, 1 in left baseline corner — horizontally even with the basket, near the 3pt corner.
3
4 comes up from the strong-side block to the strong-side elbow. This gives a high-low triangle on the right side: 5 (top) → 4 (elbow) → 2 (corner). Princeton's primary structure.
4
Paint is empty. No one in the lane. This is intentional — creates the drive lanes and backdoor cuts that Princeton lives on.
Coach's EyeThe high-low on the strong side (5 top, 4 elbow, 2 corner) means the defense has to guard three levels simultaneously. They can't help on all three. One will be open.

Dribble-At · V-Cut Backdoor · 1 & 2 Raise

1
5 dribbles directly at 3 in the weak-side top slot. This is a deliberate Princeton "dribble-at" action — it signals the backdoor and forces the defender to react.
2
3 reads the dribble-at and executes the V-cut: one step toward the ball (selling the catch), then plants and cuts BACKDOOR to the basket. The step toward is critical — it creates the separation.
3
Simultaneously: 1 raises from the left baseline corner to FT-line depth on the left side. 2 raises from the right baseline corner to FT-line depth on the right side. Both corners lift at the same time.
4
4 holds the strong-side elbow — stationary. The paint is still protected for 3's backdoor cut path.
For ParentsThe "dribble-at" is like setting a pick with the dribble — the driver goes toward the wing player, and the wing player uses that movement to back-cut to the basket. It's one of the oldest and most effective actions in basketball.

Dribble Handoff → Strong-Side Wing

1
3's backdoor is not available (defense recovered). 5 continues the dribble past 3's cleared spot, arriving at the weak-side top area. 1 (now at left FT-depth) comes to meet 5.
2
Dribble Handoff (DHO): 5 extends the ball as 1 rubs off 5's body. 1 receives the ball mid-action — this is a live dribble handoff, not a pass. The rub creates a natural screen.
3
After the DHO, 1 dribbles right, back to the strong-side wing. This re-establishes the ball on the strong side — exactly where the Chin action initiates.
4
5 pops to the top of the key after the handoff. 2 stays at right FT-depth (right wing). 4 holds strong elbow. The Chin set is now ready.
🔗 Why the DHOThe DHO is not just a ball-movement action — it's a defensive disruptor. It forces a switch decision, often creating a mismatch. 1 comes off the DHO with a full head of steam toward the strong side, putting pressure on whichever defender picks them up.

Princeton CHIN Action

A
CHIN Entry: 1 passes to 4 at the strong-side elbow. 4 catches, shot-fakes, pivots. 2 curls off 4's body from the wing toward the basket — reading: layup if open, pull-up at the elbow if defense recovers.
B
4 Drives: If 4 catches at the elbow and 2's curl draws the help, 4 drives the open elbow lane. The paint is empty — no help inside. Finish or kick to 5 at the top.
C
Skip to 3: If the strong side is fully loaded with defenders, 1 skips to 3 in the weak-side corner. 3 is open because all attention went to the Chin action. Catch-and-shoot corner 3.
D
5 Safety: 5 at the top is always the reset. If nothing develops, reverse to 5, reset, and run the Chin again from the other side.
Why CHIN Works HereThe full sequence (break → 4-out set → dribble-at → DHO → Chin) has tired the defense with decisions on every phase. By the time 4 catches at the elbow for the Chin, the defense is a step slow and has already reacted to four different actions. The Chin finishes a scheme, not just a play.
