Maryland · Box Set → Wing Ball-Screen Series

Team Melo 16U · Half-Court

4 Phases

Box Set: Down Screens

1
4 and 5 start at the elbows in box formation — 4 at the left elbow, 5 at the right elbow. Both bigs simultaneously step down toward their respective blocks to set down screens.
2
2 uses 5's screen and flashes to the right wing. 3 uses 4's screen and flashes to the left wing. Both wings cut simultaneously — the box opens up both sides.
3
1 reads both wings as the screens are set. Ball can go to either wing — choose based on who gets cleaner separation. Cue: "Read the screen — pick your side."
4
Common error: Wings cutting before the screen is set. The screener must make contact first — read the defender, then go. Cutting early gives the defense time to recover. Cue: "Wait for it — then explode."
Coach's EyeThe box set is an information play as much as a scoring play. The defense has to declare early — show their coverage on both sides. 1 reads that declaration and enters the ball to the wing with the cleaner look. This forces the defense to commit before the real action starts.
🔗 Into Phase 2Whichever wing catches the entry pass becomes the ball side. The opposite wing is now weak side. Everything downstream — where 1 cuts, where 4 steps — is determined by that entry side. The play is symmetric: the diagrams show a right-side entry, but the left side runs identically.

Wing Entry + Cuts

1
1 passes to 2 on the right wing and immediately cuts hard through the paint to the ball-side corner. The cut is not optional — it clears the top of the key and creates the spacing for the ball-screen action.
2
4 (weak-side big) steps a few paces from the left elbow toward the left short corner. This removes the weak-side help defender from the paint and opens the driving lane. 4 stays active — eyes on the ball.
3
5 holds the right block and reads 1's cut. 5 does not move until 1 has completely passed through the paint. Patience here is critical — moving too early congests the lane 1 is cutting through.
4
Common error: 1 stopping in the lane or slowing at the elbow. The cut must go all the way to the corner — a half-cut leaves a defender in position to help on the upcoming drive. Cue: "All the way — corner or bust."
Coach's Eye1's cut and 4's short-corner step happen simultaneously and serve the same purpose: remove bodies from the paint. The ball-screen action in Phase 3 only works if the key is clear. These two cuts are the setup for the setup.
🔗 Into Phase 3Once 1 is in the corner and 4 is at the short corner, 5 gets the trigger — it is time to set the ball screen. The court is now configured: wing with the ball, two bigs in position, two outlets on the weak side. The ball-screen action can begin.

Ball Screen Action: Primary

1
5 comes up from the right block and sets a ball screen for 2. The screen should be set with 5's back toward the key — forcing the defender to fight over or under. 5 must be set before 2 makes the move.
2
2 uses the screen and drives into the key. Primary look is the layup. If no layup, execute a two-foot jump stop in the paint — this freezes the defense and gives time to read all options. Cue: "Two feet — then decide."
3
Read order from the jump stop: First look — layup (take it if it is there). Second look — 5 rolling to the basket (give 5 the ball if the help collapses). Third look — 1 lifting from the corner to free throw line extended (kick out if the lane is clogged).
4
1 lifts from the ball-side corner to FT-line extended simultaneously with 5's screen. This timing is the key — 1 must be arriving at FT-line extended as 2 is making the jump-stop decision. Cue: "Screen goes up — 1 comes up."
5
Common error: 2 taking an off-balance or forced shot off the dribble without reading the defense. The jump stop exists to eliminate this. A bad shot here kills the entire possession — jump stop first, then shoot or pass from a controlled position.
Coach's Eye — The Jump Stop ReadThe jump stop is the most important technical skill in this play. It eliminates poor decisions made at speed. When 2 lands on two feet, every defender has to freeze — they do not know if 2 is shooting, passing, or driving. That freeze is the advantage. Train the jump stop until it is automatic. The reads only work if the stop is there first.
🔗 Into Phase 4If no shot is available, 2 kicks to 1 at FT-line extended. 1 does not hold the ball — it swings immediately to 3 on the left wing. The moment the ball moves left, the roles flip: 3 becomes the ball handler, 4 comes up to screen, and 2 flows to the new ball-side corner. Same action, other side.

Swing and Repeat

1
2 kicks to 1 at free throw line extended. 1 swings immediately to 3 on the left wing. No hesitation — quick ball movement before the defense can recover and rotate. Cue: "Swing, swing — don't hold it."
2
2 flows from the key to the new ball-side (left) corner immediately after kicking to 1. This is mandatory — the corner must be filled before 4 sets the screen. A player standing in the lane stops the action before it starts.
3
4 reads the swing pass and comes up from the left short corner to set the ball screen for 3. This is the mirror of what 5 did on the right side. Identical mechanics — back to the key, screen set before 3 attacks. Cue: "New side, same play."
4
5 settles to the right short corner after its roll in Phase 3. This occupies the right-side help defender and maintains spacing while the left-side action develops. 5 stays active — ready to receive if the defense forgets about them.
5
Common error: 2 standing still at the key after kicking out, or 4 hesitating before coming up to screen. The entire effectiveness of this play depends on tempo. Each player's movement triggers the next player's movement — hesitation breaks the chain.
Coach's Eye — Defending the RepeatThis is why the play is difficult to guard: it forces the defense to make all the same rotations twice, from opposite sides, in quick succession. The first action tires the defenders on the right. The swing resets to the left before they can recover. The defense that stops the first ball-screen often gives up the second one. Keep the pace — do not let them catch their breath.
